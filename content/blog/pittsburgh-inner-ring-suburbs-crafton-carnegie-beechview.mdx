When most out-of-state investors hear "Pittsburgh real estate," they think of Lawrenceville or East Liberty — the neighborhoods that get press coverage. Those submarkets are real, but by the time an area is getting national write-ups, the best entry points are gone.

We focus on Pittsburgh's **inner-ring suburbs**: the dense, transit-connected neighborhoods and boroughs within 5–8 miles of downtown that still offer an affordable cost basis with the fundamentals of a maturing market.

## What makes an inner-ring submarket

Inner-ring boroughs and neighborhoods share a few defining characteristics:

- **Pre-WWII housing stock** with durable construction and architectural character
- **Established renter base** — not transient student renters, but workforce and professional households
- **Direct transit access** to downtown via bus or busway
- **Walkable main streets** with retail, dining, and community anchors
- **Below-market purchase prices** relative to income levels

These aren't distressed areas. They're stable, unglamorous neighborhoods that generate consistent rent and low vacancy.

## Crafton Borough

Crafton sits directly on the West Busway — Pittsburgh's dedicated bus rapid transit corridor that delivers riders to downtown in under 20 minutes. The borough has a walkable main street on Crafton Avenue, owner-occupied single-family homes mixed with small multifamily, and a renter base anchored by workers commuting to Oakland and downtown employment centers.

Median home prices in Crafton remain well below the city average, and the rent-to-price ratio is among the strongest in the metro. This is where our first acquisition, 42 Baldwick Rd, is located.

## Carnegie Borough

Carnegie is on the South Busway corridor — similar transit dynamics to Crafton but on the opposite side of the city. It has a slightly larger commercial corridor on East Main Street and a mix of Victorian-era single-family and small multifamily. Carnegie also benefits from its proximity to Carnegie Mellon's Silicon Valley recruiting pipeline — graduates who can't afford San Francisco are landing jobs with CMU spinouts and choosing Pittsburgh's affordable neighborhoods.

## Beechview and Brookline

These South Hills neighborhoods are accessible via the light rail system (the T), which runs directly into downtown Pittsburgh. They've been stable working-class communities for decades — exactly the kind of demographic that generates reliable tenancy without the volatility of student or luxury rental markets.

Beechview in particular has seen quiet appreciation as buyers priced out of South Side and Mt. Washington move further along the transit corridor.

## McKees Rocks and Stowe Township

These boroughs northwest of the city represent the early innings of what Crafton was a few years ago — transit access, strong fundamentals, and prices that haven't yet caught up. Higher risk tolerance required, but the rent-to-price ratios are compelling for investors with operational capacity.

## What we look for in any submarket

Regardless of the specific neighborhood, our underwriting criteria don't change:

- **Transit access** within 0.5 miles — reduces vacancy risk structurally
- **Market occupancy above 93%** — indicates supply/demand balance
- **Rent-to-price ratio that supports day-one cash flow** at market rates
- **Local operator relationships** for property management and maintenance
- **No pending zoning or infrastructure changes** that could shift the neighborhood character

Pittsburgh's inner-ring suburbs check all of these boxes at price points that major markets can't offer. That's the opportunity — and it won't last forever.
