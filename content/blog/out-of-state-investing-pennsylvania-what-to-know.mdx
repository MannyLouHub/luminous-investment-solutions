Pennsylvania is attracting more out-of-state real estate investors than ever. Low prices, strong fundamentals, and a stable economic base are bringing capital from New York, New Jersey, California, and Florida into Pennsylvania's metros large and small.

Some of those investors are doing well. Others are learning expensive lessons. The difference usually comes down to preparation.

Here's what out-of-state buyers consistently underestimate when entering Pennsylvania — and what you should have in place before you close.

## 1. You need a local property manager from day one

Pennsylvania's housing stock is old. In many of our markets, the median home was built before 1950. Maintenance needs are different from a 2005 construction in Phoenix — you'll encounter knob-and-tube wiring, aging boilers, lead paint, and deferred maintenance that a walk-through inspection won't fully surface.

Managing a Pennsylvania property from out of state without a trusted local property manager is how investors get burned. You need someone who knows which contractors are reliable, which code requirements are actively enforced in each borough, and when a repair is a quick fix vs. a capital expenditure.

Good property managers in these markets run **8–10% of gross rents** for full-service management. Budget for it from day one, not as an afterthought.

## 2. Insurance is harder than you expect

Pennsylvania insurers are increasingly cautious about older housing stock. Knob-and-tube wiring, galvanized pipes, and flat roofs can result in coverage exclusions, higher premiums, or outright denials. Some insurers require updated electrical and plumbing before they'll bind a policy.

Get an insurance quote **before** you finalize your offer — not during the inspection period. A property that pencils at $1,200/year in insurance may not pencil at $3,500/year with the exclusions a realistic policy carries.

Work with an independent broker who knows the local market and has placed policies on comparable properties nearby.

## 3. The inspection matters more here than almost anywhere

Many buyers try to waive inspections in competitive markets. Don't do this in Pennsylvania.

Pre-war construction can hide significant issues: failing chimney liners, deteriorated drain lines (common in cast-iron and clay tile systems), active knob-and-tube wiring, and structural settlement from the hilly terrain and variable soil conditions common across Western Pennsylvania.

Beyond the general inspection, budget for:
- **Sewer scope** — $150–$200, worth it on any property built before 1980
- **Lead paint inspection** if you plan to rent to families with children (required disclosure; XRF testing recommended)
- **Radon test** — Pennsylvania has elevated radon levels; testing is mandatory in some transaction contexts and wise in all of them

## 4. Borough-level rules are real

Pennsylvania is one of the most politically fragmented states in the country — some counties alone contain well over 100 municipalities. Each has its own zoning code, rental registration requirements, certificate of occupancy standards, and code enforcement culture.

One borough's rental registration process is different from the next. A neighboring municipality's inspection requirements can be completely different. What's allowed by right in one municipality may require a variance in the next.

Before you buy, confirm:
- Whether the property's current use is permitted under the local zoning code
- Whether a rental registration or certificate of occupancy is required and what the inspection entails
- Whether there are any open code violations or municipal liens on the property (request a municipal lien search)

A local real estate attorney can pull this information in a few hours. Spend the money — it's far cheaper than discovering a compliance issue after closing.

## 5. Title insurance is not optional

Pennsylvania has a history of heir's property issues, particularly in older urban neighborhoods. Properties can have unclear chain-of-title dating back decades. Municipal liens, unpaid utilities, and estate complications can all attach to title.

Use a local title company. Pay for the extended coverage endorsement. This is not a place to save $500.

## 6. Build your team before you make offers

The investors who do well in Pennsylvania markets are the ones who arrive with relationships, not the ones who build them reactively under contract. Before you make your first offer, have:

- A local real estate attorney familiar with residential investment transactions
- A title company or settlement agent
- A property inspector with experience in pre-WWII housing
- A property manager with existing local inventory
- An independent insurance broker
- A local lender (or a national lender with a track record in PA markets)

This sounds like a lot of work before you've found a deal. It is. But having this infrastructure in place means you can move quickly when the right property appears — and you won't be scrambling to find a contractor when something breaks 90 days after closing.

## The case for doing it right

Pennsylvania is a legitimate opportunity for long-term investors who are willing to put in the preparation work. The market rewards those who understand it and punishes those who treat it like a low-risk entry point because prices are low.

Low prices mean old stock, complex local rules, and thin margins that demand careful execution. Get that right and the fundamentals will work for you.
