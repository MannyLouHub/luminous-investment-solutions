Every real estate investor knows that rental demand follows jobs. What fewer investors think carefully about is *what kind of jobs* — because not all employment is created equal when it comes to housing stability.

Pittsburgh's economy is anchored by what urban economists call the "Eds & Meds" sector: education and healthcare. It's not a flashy story, but it's one of the most durable foundations a rental market can have.

## The anchor institutions

The Pittsburgh metro is home to some of the most significant institutions in the country:

- **Carnegie Mellon University** — globally ranked in computer science, engineering, and business; a leading producer of AI and robotics talent
- **University of Pittsburgh** — one of the top research universities in the U.S., with a medical school and health system that employs tens of thousands
- **UPMC (University of Pittsburgh Medical Center)** — the region's largest employer, with 92,000+ employees across 40+ hospitals and hundreds of outpatient facilities
- **Allegheny Health Network** — the second major health system, adding tens of thousands more healthcare workers to the metro
- **Duquesne University and Robert Morris University** — additional anchors in the city proper

Together, these institutions employ well over 100,000 people in the Pittsburgh metro — and that number grows year over year regardless of what the broader economy is doing.

## Why this matters for rental demand

Healthcare workers and university employees share a few key characteristics that make them ideal tenants:

**Stable employment.** Hospitals don't downsize during recessions — they get busier. University enrollment tracks long-term demographic trends, not quarterly earnings. These jobs don't evaporate in downturns.

**Geographic anchoring.** A UPMC surgeon or a CMU professor can't work remotely and can't move their employer to a lower-cost city. They live where the institution is. This creates durable, location-specific rental demand that doesn't respond to national economic cycles the way finance or tech employment does.

**Income range.** Eds & Meds jobs span every income level — from first-year medical residents to tenured faculty to hospital administrators. This breadth supports demand across the full rental spectrum, from workforce housing to professional apartments.

## Pittsburgh vs. tech-dependent markets

Compare Pittsburgh to a market like Austin or Phoenix, where explosive job growth has been driven primarily by tech sector expansion and relocation incentives. Those markets can add 30,000 jobs in a good year — and lose 15,000 in a correction.

Pittsburgh's job growth is slower and less dramatic. But it's also more predictable. The metro has added 15,000–20,000 jobs per year consistently over the last several years, with the healthcare and education sectors as the primary driver.

For a real estate investor with a 5–10 year hold horizon, predictability beats peak growth. You want a market where you can model vacancy conservatively and have confidence the assumptions hold.

## The CMU effect

One under-appreciated dynamic in Pittsburgh is the long-term impact of CMU's AI and robotics program. Pittsburgh is now home to one of the highest concentrations of AI researchers and engineers in the world — with companies like Uber ATG (now Aurora), Argo AI, and dozens of CMU spinouts having established significant Pittsburgh footprints.

These companies create high-income, educated renters who may not want to own but can afford top-of-market rents. They also tend to be in Pittsburgh specifically because of the CMU pipeline — meaning the demand is anchored, not footloose.

## The bottom line

When we underwrote our commitment to Pittsburgh, the Eds & Meds foundation was a central part of the thesis. It's not the most exciting headline, but it's the kind of structural demand driver that lets you underwrite conservatively and still generate solid returns.

Markets built on tourism, retail, or single-industry employers carry embedded volatility that most projections underweight. Pittsburgh's anchor institutions have been operating for over a century and have every incentive to keep growing.
